Landforms & Bodies of Water

Mountains, Rivers, Oceans, and More • Printable Web Lesson

Earth has many natural features. Landforms and bodies of water help us describe what places look like and how people live there.

What Students Will Learn

Students identify common landforms and bodies of water, compare features such as mountains and valleys, and explain how coasts and islands shape human choices.

Learning Targets

  • I can define landform and body of water.
  • I can compare mountains, hills, valleys, and plains.
  • I can describe rivers, lakes, oceans, coasts, and islands.
  • I can use geography vocabulary to design an imaginary place.

Student Directions

  • Read the section and study the image before answering.
  • Use the vocabulary and quick facts to support your thinking.
  • Write in complete sentences unless the prompt says bullets are okay.
  • On paper: circle, label, or underline important evidence in the print image.

Key Vocabulary

Landform
A natural shape on Earth’s surface.
Body of water
A water feature such as a river, lake, or ocean.
Coast
The area where land meets the ocean.
Island
Land that has water all around it.

Section 1: What Is a Landform?

A landform is a natural shape on Earth’s surface. Some landforms rise high, some are flat, and some are surrounded by water.

Quick Facts

  • Mountains rise high above nearby land.
  • Plains are broad and mostly flat.
  • Deserts, valleys, hills, and islands are landforms too.

Look Closely

  • Find high land and low land.
  • Look for where land meets water.
  • Think about what might be hard or easy to cross.

Prompt A — Name the Landform

Describe it

Choose one landform and describe what it looks like.

Section 2: Mountains, Hills, and Valleys

Some land rises high, and some land dips low between higher places. Comparing shapes helps geographers describe a place.

Quick Facts

  • A mountain is high and often steep.
  • A hill is raised land that is usually lower than a mountain.
  • A valley is lower land between hills or mountains.

Compare

  • Which feature is highest?
  • Which feature is lowest?
  • Where might water flow?

Landforms can affect roads, weather, farms, and where people build communities.

Prompt B — Compare

Use two features

How are a mountain and a valley different?

Section 3: Rivers, Lakes, and Oceans

Bodies of water can be small or huge, still or moving, fresh or salty. They provide resources and travel routes.

Vocabulary

  • River: moving water that flows across land.
  • Lake: water surrounded by land.
  • Ocean: a huge body of salty water.

Image Mission

  • Look for moving water or still water.
  • Notice nearby landforms.
  • Ask how people might use this water.

Prompt C — Water Clues

3 examples

Explain how a river, lake, and ocean are different.

Section 4: Coasts and Islands

A coast is where land meets the ocean, and an island has water around it. People plan carefully in these places.

Examples

  • Coasts may have beaches, cliffs, ports, or wetlands.
  • Islands can be large or small.
  • Weather and waves can change coastal places.

Think Like a Geographer

  • How would people travel here?
  • Where would buildings be safest?
  • What resources might people use?

Prompt D — Coast or Island?

One paragraph

Describe a coast or island and name one challenge people might plan for there.

Section 5: Quick Check + Build a Model

Landforms and water features help us describe real places and imagine new ones.

Mini Quiz

  • Define landform.
  • Name two bodies of water.
  • What makes an island an island?

Challenge

  • Invent an island.
  • Add three landforms and two bodies of water.
  • Explain how people could travel or find water there.

Prompt E — Summary

5–6 sentences

Summarize four landforms or bodies of water from this lesson.

Prompt F — Create

Bullets OK

Draw or describe an imaginary island with three landforms and two bodies of water.

Food Chains

Food Chains • Printable Web Lesson

On screen this looks like a modern lesson page — but when you print it, it becomes a worksheet. Read, observe, then write your explanations in complete sentences.

What Students Will Learn

Students learn how energy moves through a food chain: producers make food, consumers eat plants or animals, and decomposers break down dead material. Each section builds vocabulary and explanation skills.

Learning Targets

  • I can identify a producer, consumer, and decomposer.
  • I can explain how energy moves in a food chain.
  • I can describe what happens when one part of a food chain changes.
  • I can use food-chain vocabulary correctly in a sentence.

Student Directions

  • Read each section and study the picture.
  • Use evidence from the quick facts to answer.
  • Write complete sentences when asked.
  • On paper: underline key vocabulary words.

Key Vocabulary

Producer
a plant that makes its own food using sunlight.
Consumer
an animal that gets energy by eating plants or animals.
Decomposer
an organism that breaks down dead plants and animals.
Energy
the power living things use to grow, move, and survive.

Section 1: Producers: where food chains begin

Producers are usually plants. They use sunlight, water, and air to make food.

Quick Facts

  • Plants are producers.
  • Producers capture sunlight.
  • All food chains start with producers.

Look Closely

  • Find a plant in the image.
  • What does it need to make food?
  • What might eat this plant?

Prompt A — Identify

2–3 sentences

Explain why the plant is a producer. What makes it different from an animal?

Section 2: Consumers: animals that eat

Consumers get energy by eating plants or other animals. Some are herbivores, carnivores, or omnivores.

Types

  • Herbivore: eats plants
  • Carnivore: eats animals
  • Omnivore: eats both

Look Closely

  • What might this animal eat?
  • Is it a herbivore or carnivore?
  • What clues help you decide?

Use evidence from the picture and the facts.

Prompt B — Classify

Fill both lines

This animal is a (herbivore / carnivore / omnivore) because: ____________ One thing it might eat is: ____________

Section 3: Food chains in habitats

Habitats provide the plants and animals needed to form food chains.

Vocabulary

  • Habitat: where organisms live
  • Population: a group of the same kind
  • Community: all living things in an area

Image Mission

  • Name 2 living things you see.
  • What could eat what?
  • Where does the chain start?

Prompt C — Build a Chain

3–4 sentences

Write a simple food chain using three living things (plant → animal → animal). Explain the order.

Section 4: What happens if something changes?

If one part of a food chain changes (less food, fewer predators), other parts are affected too.

Examples

  • Less rain → fewer plants
  • Fewer plants → fewer herbivores
  • Fewer herbivores → fewer carnivores

Think Like a Scientist

  • What changed?
  • Who is affected first?
  • Who is affected next?

Prompt D — Cause & Effect

One paragraph

If a habitat has less water, how might that affect a food chain? Explain step by step.

Section 5: Quick Check + Create

Use the words producer, consumer, decomposer, and energy.

Mini Quiz

  • What is a producer?
  • What is a consumer?
  • What do decomposers do?

Challenge

  • Invent a habitat.
  • Add a producer and two consumers.
  • Explain the energy path.

Prompt E — Summary

5–6 sentences

Write a summary explaining producers, consumers, decomposers, and energy in a food chain.

Prompt F — Create a Food Chain

Bullets OK

Habitat: ____________ Producer: ____________ Consumer #1: ____________ Consumer #2: ____________ Energy path: ____________

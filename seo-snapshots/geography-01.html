Maps & Globes

Tools for Finding Places • Printable Web Lesson

Geographers use maps, globes, symbols, and directions to understand where places are and how to move between them.

What Students Will Learn

Students learn how maps and globes represent Earth, how symbols and legends help readers understand places, and how cardinal directions make routes easier to explain.

Learning Targets

  • I can explain what maps and globes show.
  • I can compare a flat map with a round globe.
  • I can use symbols, keys, and legends to read a map.
  • I can describe a route using north, south, east, and west.

Student Directions

  • Read the section and study the image before answering.
  • Use the vocabulary and quick facts to support your thinking.
  • Write in complete sentences unless the prompt says bullets are okay.
  • On paper: circle, label, or underline important evidence in the print image.

Key Vocabulary

Map
A drawing that shows places from above.
Globe
A small round model of Earth.
Legend
A key that explains map symbols.
Compass rose
A symbol that shows north, south, east, and west.

Section 1: What Maps Show

A map is a drawing that shows places from above. Maps can show land, water, roads, buildings, borders, and routes.

Quick Facts

  • Maps show places from a top-down view.
  • Different maps can show different kinds of information.
  • A map title tells the reader what the map is about.

Look Closely

  • Find a route, a place name, and a symbol.
  • Notice what is close together and what is far apart.
  • Ask what the map helps someone do.

Prompt A — Read a Map

2–3 sentences

Name three things a map can show and explain how they help people.

Section 2: Globes Show Earth

A globe is shaped like Earth, so it helps us see how land and water wrap around the planet.

Quick Facts

  • A globe is round like Earth.
  • Globes show continents, oceans, and countries.
  • A globe is useful for seeing the whole planet.

Compare

  • A map is flat and easy to carry.
  • A globe is round and shows Earth’s shape.
  • Both maps and globes help us find places.

Maps and globes are tools. The best tool depends on the question you are trying to answer.

Prompt B — Map or Globe?

Compare

Explain one way a map and a globe are alike and one way they are different.

Section 3: Symbols, Keys, and Legends

Map symbols stand for real things like roads, rivers, parks, schools, airports, and capital cities.

Vocabulary

  • Symbol: a small picture or mark with a meaning.
  • Legend: the part of a map that explains symbols.
  • Scale: a clue for real distances on a map.

Map Mission

  • Choose two symbols and predict what they mean.
  • Find the legend or key.
  • Use the key to check your thinking.

Prompt C — Use the Key

2 examples

Choose two map symbols and tell what each one means.

Section 4: Directions and Compass Rose

A compass rose helps readers find north, south, east, and west. Directions make routes clear.

Quick Facts

  • North and south point up and down on many maps.
  • East and west point side to side on many maps.
  • Directions can be combined, such as northeast.

Think Like a Geographer

  • Start with a clear beginning place.
  • Use direction words in order.
  • Check that another reader could follow your route.

Prompt D — Give Directions

Route writing

Write directions from one place to another using north, south, east, or west.

Section 5: Quick Check + Create a Map

Maps help people organize information about places. A good map has a title, symbols, a legend, and directions.

Mini Quiz

  • What is one thing a map can show?
  • How is a globe different from a map?
  • What does a legend explain?

Challenge

  • Design a classroom or neighborhood map.
  • Add a title and at least three symbols.
  • Create a key that explains the symbols.

Prompt E — Summary

5–6 sentences

Summarize how maps, globes, symbols, and directions help people.

Prompt F — Create

Bullets OK

Design a classroom or neighborhood map with a title, symbols, and a key.
